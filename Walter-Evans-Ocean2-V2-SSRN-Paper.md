**ABSTRACT**

The Ocean-2 V2 represents a significant advancement in wave energy converter (WEC) technology, designed to address the "energy transfer gap" through integrated onboard green hydrogen conversion. This paper details the evolution of the Ocean-2 from its initial 50 KW prototype tested in Puget Sound to the current V2 design, which is rated for a maximum capacity of 600 KW. The V2 design utilizes a hybrid "bobbing ball" overtopping mechanism, which captures energy from vertical wave motion (heaving) and subsequent water drainage through internal hydro-turbines. By converting generated electricity into exportable green hydrogen via seawater electrolysis, the Ocean-2 V2 bypasses the need for costly subsea cabling, enabling scalable deep-ocean deployment. This study analyzes the mechanical blueprint, performance metrics, and the 2030 vision for large-scale wave energy arrays.

**INTRODUCTION**

Wave energy is one of the most concentrated forms of renewable energy, yet it remains largely untapped due to the challenges of deep-ocean deployment and efficient energy transmission. Traditional WECs are often restricted to coastal areas where they compete for space and face significant ecological scrutiny. The Ocean-2 V2, developed by Panthalassa, introduces a modular, deep-ocean solution that minimizes ecological impact while maximizing energy harvest.

**LITERATURE REVIEW AND PRIOR ART**

Current WEC designs generally fall into categories such as point absorbers, oscillating water columns (OWC), and overtopping devices. Point absorbers, like those reviewed by Guo et al. (2022), are efficient in deep water but often suffer from complex mechanical linkages. Overtopping devices traditionally rely on large, shore-bound reservoirs. The Ocean-2 V2 innovates by miniaturizing the overtopping cycle into a 10-meter wide modular sphere, combining the high-energy capture of a point absorber with the mechanical simplicity of an overtopping turbine.

**THE MECHANICAL BLUEPRINT AND INNOVATION**

The core of the Ocean-2 V2 is the "bobbing ball" mechanism. As waves pass, the spherical buoyant body bobs up and down, forcing seawater up a submerged vertical tube. This water is collected in an internal reservoir within the sphere and then drained through high-efficiency hydro-turbines.

Key technical specifications include:
*   **Maximum Rated Power:** 600 KW.
*   **Prototype Output:** 50 KW (Puget Sound demonstration).
*   **Modular Construction:** Built using rust-proof alloys and advanced composites.
*   **Digital Infrastructure:** Integrated Oceans 2.0 API for real-time monitoring and alert-derived analytics.

**ONBOARD GREEN HYDROGEN CONVERSION**

One of the most significant hurdles for offshore energy is the cost of subsea power cables, which can account for up to 30% of total project costs. The Ocean-2 V2 solves this by integrating seawater electrolysis. The electricity generated by the internal turbines is immediately used to split seawater into hydrogen and oxygen. The resulting green hydrogen can be stored and transported via tankers, transforming the WEC into a mobile energy production hub.

**ECOLOGICAL IMPACT AND SUSTAINABILITY**

The Ocean-2 V2 was developed in collaboration with marine biologists to ensure a quiet, uninvasive design. Satellite tracking is used to monitor impact on marine biodiversity, ensuring that large-scale arrays do not disrupt local ecosystems. This "minimal impact" approach is critical for gaining regulatory approval and public support for deep-ocean deployment.

**2030 VISION: LARGE-SCALE ARRAYS**

The ultimate goal for the Ocean-2 technology is the deployment of large-scale arrays capable of powering entire coastal cities. By 2030, Panthalassa aims to have multiple arrays operational in deep-ocean sites, contributing significantly to the global Blue Economy and the transition to carbon-neutral energy.

**CONCLUSION**

The Ocean-2 V2 provides a viable pathway for scaling wave energy. By combining a robust mechanical design with innovative energy storage solutions like green hydrogen, it addresses the primary technical and economic barriers to deep-ocean energy harvesting.
